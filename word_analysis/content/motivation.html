This investigation into the multiplicity of perspectives within Levinas's text is motivated out of a concern that too much of the secondary literature employs one-dimensional analyses of Levinas's work, an analysis which mistakenly reduces his thinking to hierarchical calculations of rank. This hierarchcal reading is widespread throughout both laudatory and critical readings of Levinas, and more generally within the much of the analytic literature.

The unhealthy degree of reverence with which Levinas has read been by his admirers read has long been criticized. In 2004, Simon Critchely lamented

Two years later, Samuel Moyn offered a similar assessment

Why does Levinas interpretation tend to attract such readings of his work? What impels certain commntators to position themselves as submissive to his higher power? Much of the fault can be attributed to the seductiveness of Levinas's language: if one can manage to wend one's way through the contortions of verbiage, it is hard not to be enchanted by its strange poetry, hypnotised by its rhythm, mesmerized by its an various intensities and extremities.

But, as anyone who has struggled through these texts would know, then a problem occurs: how can one start to understand these texts that are so seductive yet so difficult to understand? Before we present various options alongside our own solution, which is the mission of this project, we wish to remark that Levinas deliberately writes cryptic prose. Throughout his analyses of manifold phenomenon, he reiterates the essentially 'hidden' or 'clandestine' aspect of that which is shown: we can know something from the outside as something known to us, but we can never completely know what that thing is from the inside. No matter how much knowledge you accumulate about another person, it's impossible to ever really know what it's like to be that person. An object present its observable sides to us, but inso doing they hide its reverse. Materiality itself presents itself as an obscurity. We can understand matter in its forms and even the formal properties of matter but what matter is in itself has never been entirely clear: it's there but what is it? Levinas approaches language in much the same fashion: simultaneusly presenting characteristics of an event while explaining that these same characteristics conceal something unpresentable.
